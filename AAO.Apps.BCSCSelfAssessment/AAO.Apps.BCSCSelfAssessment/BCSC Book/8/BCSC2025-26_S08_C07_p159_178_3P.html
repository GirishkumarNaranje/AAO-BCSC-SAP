Anomalies of Size and Shape of the Cornea

The horizontal dia­meter of a normal cornea is approximately 10 mm in neonates and ranges from 10.5–12.5 mm ­after 2 years of age. ­Table 7-2 summarizes key ­measurements in normal and anomalous corneas.

Microcornea

Microcornea refers to a clear cornea of normal thickness with a horizontal dia­meter of less than 9 mm at birth or less than 10 mm ­after 2 years of age (Fig 7-1). It is impor­tant to distinguish microcornea from microphthalmos (a small globe with anatomical malformations) and nanophthalmos (a small but structurally normal globe). Ultrasonography and axial length ­measurements can be helpful in differentiating ­these conditions. In microphthalmos and nanophthalmos, the axial length is 2 SDs below the mean length for age (typically <18–20 mm). The etiology of microcornea is unknown, but it may be related to fetal arrest of corneal growth in the fifth month of gestation. Alternatively, it may be related to overgrowth of the anterior tips of the optic cup, which leaves less space for the cornea to develop. Microcornea is inherited as an autosomal dominant (most commonly) or recessive trait and is associated with a pathogenic variant (mutation) in the PAX6 gene. ­There is no sex predilection. ­Because the cornea is relatively flat in microcornea, ­these eyes are usually hyperopic, and ­there is an increased incidence of angle-­closure glaucoma. Open-­angle glaucoma develops ­later in life in 20% of patients in whom angle-­closure glaucoma does not occur.

Impor­tant ocular anomalies often associated with microcornea include per­sis­tent fetal vasculature (PFV), congenital cataracts, anterior segment dysgenesis, glaucoma, and optic nerve hypoplasia. Significant systemic associations include fetal alcohol syndrome, Ehlers-­Danlos syndrome, and achondroplasia.

When microcornea occurs as an isolated finding, the visual prognosis is excellent if spectacles are used to treat the hyperopia resulting from the flat cornea.

Megalocornea

Megalocornea is a bilateral, nonprogressive enlargement of the cornea. On histologic examination, the cornea is normal but ­measures 13 mm or more in dia­meter (or >12 mm in an infant) (Fig 7-2). ­There are 2 forms of this anomaly. In primary megalocornea, the enlarged cornea is an isolated finding with no associated ocular or systemic anomalies. The second form is megalocornea with associated ocular and systemic anomalies.

The etiology may be related to failure of the optic cup to grow and of its anterior tips to close, leaving a larger space for the cornea to fill. Alternatively, megalocornea may represent arrested buphthalmos (enlargement of the entire globe) and exaggerated growth of the cornea in relation to the rest of the eye. An abnormality in collagen production is suggested by the association of megalocornea with systemic disorders of collagen synthesis (eg, Marfan syndrome). Males are affected more often than females, ­because megalocornea is often inherited as an X-­linked recessive trait. The corneal dia­meter may also be slightly increased in heterozygous females. X-­linked cases are due to pathogenic variants in the chordin-­like 1 gene (CHRDL1), which encodes for ventroptin, an antagonist of bone morphoge­ne­tic protein that is involved in corneal epithelial homeostasis.

The steep cornea in megalocornea ­causes myopia and with-­the-­rule astigmatism, which are managed with glasses and contact lenses.

Ocular anomalies associated with megalocornea include cataract, ectopia lentis, iris translucency, miosis, pigment dispersion, goniodysgenesis, arcus juvenilis, and glaucoma (but not congenital glaucoma).

Nonocular and systemic associations may include craniosynostosis, facial anomalies, intellectual disability, Down syndrome, and Marfan syndrome (see ­Table 7-1).

Megalocornea must be distinguished from buphthalmos, an enlargement of the globe caused by congenital glaucoma (discussed ­later in the chapter). Congenital glaucoma can be ruled out by the absence of elevated intraocular pressure and optic disc cupping. Other findings more typical of megalocornea than congenital glaucoma include clear cornea, pigment dispersion, iris translucency, Krukenberg spindle, absence of Haab striae, and normal axial length with a relatively increased anterior chamber depth and decreased vitreous length (may be assessed by ultrasonography).

Clinical Pearl In megalocornea, lens instability and subluxation, iridodonesis, and poor zonular fiber integrity may pre­sent additional risks and challenges in the surgical management of cataracts, including se­lection and placement of an intraocular lens (IOL) (Video 7-1).

Cornea plana

Cornea plana is a rare condition in which the cornea is abnormally flat, with typical keratometry readings of 30.00–35.00 diopters (D) (see ­Table 7-2). Additional hallmarks of cornea plana include high hyperopia (usually >10.00 D), shallow anterior chamber, and peripheral corneal haze or arcus with an indistinct limbus. Corneal curvature that is the same as the curvature of the adjacent sclera is pathognomonic of this condition. Most isolated cases of cornea plana appear in patients of Finnish ancestry.

Both autosomal recessive and autosomal dominant forms of cornea plana have been associated with pathogenic variants in the KERA gene, which codes for keratan sulfate proteoglycans (keratocan, lumican, and mimecan). ­These proteins are thought to play an impor­tant role in the regular spacing of corneal collagen fibrils. Investigators have speculated that KERA pathogenic variants cause an alteration of the tertiary structure of the keratan sulfate proteoglycans that leads to the cornea plana phenotype.

Ocular abnormalities associated with cornea plana include corneal clouding, cataracts, and anterior and posterior colobomas. ­Because of the shallow anterior chamber, angle-­closure glaucoma can occur; open-­angle glaucoma may develop ­because of ­angle abnormalities. Cornea plana is often seen in association with sclerocornea (discussed in the following section; see Fig 7-3) and with microcornea. Sclerocornea can be distinguished from cornea plana by the loss of corneal transparency and limbal architecture. Ehlers-­Danlos syndrome can also be associated with cornea plana.

Medical or surgical treatment of cornea plana consists of correction of refractive errors and control of glaucoma. Loss of central corneal clarity may require deep anterior lamellar keratoplasty (DALK) or penetrating keratoplasty (PK).

Sclerocornea

Sclerocornea is a nonprogressive, noninflammatory scleralization of the cornea. In sclerocornea, scleral tissue extends across the limbus into the peripheral cornea. The scleralization may be ­limited to the corneal periphery, or the entire cornea may be involved. The limbus is usually ill defined, and superficial vessels that are extensions of normal scleral, episcleral, and conjunctival vessels cross the corneal limbus (Fig 7-3). Sclerocornea is usually sporadic, but both autosomal dominant and autosomal recessive patterns of inheritance have been reported. No sex predilection is evident, and 90% of cases are bilateral.

The most common associated ocular anomaly is cornea plana, which occurs in 80% of cases. ­Angle structures are also commonly malformed. Multiple systemic anomalies have been reported in association with sclerocornea (see ­Table 7-1).

Keratoglobus

Keratoglobus is an ectasia of the cornea, characterized by bilateral diffuse steepening and thinning. It manifests as a globular corneal shape and is associated with Ehlers-­Danlos syndrome type VI. Unlike other corneal ectatic diseases such as keratoconus and pellucid marginal degeneration, keratoglobus is typically pre­sent at birth, although acquired forms have been reported. ­These conditions are discussed in further detail in Chapter 9.

Developmental Anomalies of the Cornea and Associated
Anterior Segment Structures

Beginning around the sixth week of gestation, anterior ocular structures are formed by 3 waves of migration of neural crest–­derived cells that differentiate into corneal endothelium, corneal stroma, and iris stroma. Disruption at any point in this ­process can hinder subsequent development and differentiation of anterior segment structures (Video 7-2). The term anterior segment dysgenesis describes a spectrum of congenital anomalies that arise from miscues during anterior segment embryogenesis and affect any or all of the anterior segment structures, including the cornea, anterior chamber ­angle, iris, and lens. High-­frequency ultrasonography can be very helpful in detecting under­lying anatomical abnormalities in ­these structures.

Posterior embryotoxon

The Schwalbe line represents the junction of the trabecular meshwork and the termination of Descemet membrane. The term posterior embryotoxon is used when the Schwalbe line is thickened and anteriorly displaced, appearing as an irregular, opaque ridge 0.5–2.0 mm anterior to the limbus. Posterior embryotoxon is vis­i­ble on external examination in 8%–30% of healthy eyes (Fig 7-4). It is usually bilateral and inherited as a dominant trait. The abnormality may occur as an isolated finding or with other anterior segment anomalies that are part of ocular or systemic syndromes, such as Axenfeld-­Rieger syndrome, arteriohepatic dysplasia (Alagille syndrome), X-­linked ichthyosis, or familial aniridia.

Axenfeld-­Rieger syndrome

Axenfeld-­Rieger syndrome (ARS) is a spectrum of disorders characterized by posterior embryotoxon with attached iris strands, iris hypoplasia, corectopia, pseudopolycoria, and glaucoma (in 50% of cases occurring in late childhood or in adulthood). Associated craniofacial, dental, skeletal, and umbilical abnormalities are often pre­sent (Fig 7-5). The conditions previously known as Axenfeld anomaly or syndrome and Rieger anomaly or syndrome, which overlapped genotypically and phenotypically, became part of the ARS spectrum.

Autosomal dominant inheritance is most common (75% of cases), but transmission can be sporadic. ARS is associated with pathogenic variants in PAX6, PITX2, and FOXC1. The differential diagnosis of ARS includes iridocorneal endothelial (ICE) syndrome; irregular iris architecture is pre­sent in both conditions, and glaucoma is associated with both conditions. However, unlike ARS, ICE is unilateral and sporadic, and the corneal endothelium is usually irregular (see Chapter 6 in this volume and BCSC Section 10, Glaucoma).

Peters anomaly

Peters anomaly (kerato-­irido-­lenticular dysgenesis) is characterized by the presence, at birth, of a central or paracentral corneal opacity (leukoma), which is due to a localized defect of the posterior stroma, Descemet membrane, and endothelium. Eighty ­percent of cases are bilateral. Most cases occur sporadically with no identifiable ge­ne­tic variant, although autosomal recessive and autosomal dominant cases have been reported. In inherited cases, pathogenic variants in PAX6, PITX2, FOXC1, and CYP1B1 disrupt the normal development of the anterior segment.

Peters anomaly is classified into 2 types:

  • Type 1 affects primarily the cornea and is characterized by iridocorneal adhesions, with iris strands extending to the edges of the corneal defect. The corneal opacity is avascular and may be central, paracentral, or total (less common) (Fig 7-6).
  • Type 2 is characterized by corneolenticular adhesions and/or cataract, along with a central or total corneal opacity that is usually vascularized (see Fig 7-6). Type 2 is presumed to be due to a developmental failure in separation of the invaginating lens vesicle from the overlying surface ectoderm.

The same pathogenic variant can cause Peters type 1 to occur in one eye and type 2 in the contralateral eye. High-­frequency ultrasonography can be very useful in differentiating between the two types.

Glaucoma is pre­sent in approximately 50% of Peters anomaly cases. Additional associated ocular abnormalities include microphthalmos, coloboma, microcornea, aniridia, ret­i­nal detachment, and PFV. Peters anomaly is a major indication for pediatric keratoplasty. The prognosis for vision rehabilitation with corneal transplantation is better for patients with Peters anomaly type 1 than for ­those with type 2 (see Chapter 16). If the central opacity is small, a less invasive surgical procedure, such as an optical iridectomy, could also be considered.

See BCSC Section 4, Ophthalmic Pathology and Intraocular Tumors; Section 6, Pediatric Ophthalmology and Strabismus; and Section 10, Glaucoma, for further discussion of Peters anomaly.

Peters-­plus syndrome Peters anomaly that is associated with systemic abnormalities is called Peters-­plus syndrome. Systemic involvement is variable and may include the following:

  • dysmorphic facial features: short palpebral fissure, cleft lip/palate, long philtrum
  • external ear abnormalities and hearing loss
  • short stature
  • intellectual disability
  • developmental milestone delays
  • heart defects
  • central ­nervous system deficits
  • gastrointestinal and genitourinary defects
  • skeletal defects

Patients with Peters-­plus syndrome should be referred to the appropriate subspecialists for the evaluation and management of systemic abnormalities.

Posterior keratoconus

Posterior keratoconus is characterized by a usually localized central or paracentral indentation of the posterior cornea without protrusion of the anterior corneal surface, as is seen in typical keratoconus (discussed in Chapter 9). Posterior excavation can occur in multiple areas; ­there is also a generalized form of this disease that can involve much of the cornea. Classification of posterior keratoconus as a congenital anomaly is supported by its association with an abnormal anterior banded layer of Descemet membrane and its presence at birth.

Posterior keratoconus may be a variant of Peters anomaly but can be distinguished by the presence of an intact Descemet membrane (focally absent in Peters anomaly). Loss of stromal tissue can lead to corneal thinning that approaches one-­third of normal (Fig 7-7); corneal tomography can confirm the corneal thinning and posterior elevation. Focal pigment deposits and guttae are often pre­sent at the margins of the opacity. Mild irregularities in anterior corneal curvature overlying the area of posterior involvement can contribute to irregular astigmatism and amblyopia, which should be assessed for and treated appropriately when pre­sent. However, irregular astigmatism is usually not as severe as can be seen in keratoconus.

Most cases of posterior keratoconus are unilateral and sporadic. Acquired posterior keratoconus can also occur, usually following trauma.

Congenital anterior staphyloma and keratectasia

Congenital anterior staphyloma is a rare developmental anomaly characterized by an opaque cornea protruding between the eyelids and partial or complete absence of Descemet membrane and endothelium (Fig 7-8A). A thin layer of uveal tissue lines the posterior cornea. The anterior segment is usually markedly abnormal, often with iridocorneal adhesions, iris hypoplasia, and lens opacity, and may result in glaucoma. Exposure may promote corneal scarring and keratinization. Inflammation is markedly absent. Unlike Peters anomaly, congenital anterior staphyloma is usually unilateral, but the contralateral eye frequently has some form of anterior segment abnormality. Typically, cases are sporadic, with no familial or systemic association.

On histologic examination, keratectasia differs from congenital anterior staphyloma only by the absence of a thin layer of uveal tissue lining the posterior cornea (Fig 7-8B), which is pre­sent in congenital anterior staphyloma. Keratectasia is possibly the result of intrauterine keratitis or vitamin deficiency and subsequent corneal perforation.

Except in very mild cases, the visual prognosis for both congenital anterior staphyloma and keratectasia is poor ­because of associated severe abnormal development of the anterior segment. PK and sclerokeratoplasty may be useful to preserve the globe and improve cosmesis; however, enucleation may be required for a painful, blind glaucomatous eye.

Secondary Abnormalities Affecting the Pediatric Cornea

Intrauterine Keratitis: Bacterial and Syphilitic

Infections acquired in utero or during delivery can cause ocular damage in several ways:

  • through direct involvement of the infectious agent, which damages tissue
  • through a teratogenic effect that results in malformation
  • through a delayed reactivation of the infectious agent ­after birth, with inflammation that damages fully developed tissue

Congenital syphilis is acquired in utero and caused by infection with the spirochete Treponema pallidum. It can lead to fetal death or premature delivery. A variety of systemic manifestations have been widely described. In ­children with untreated congenital syphilis, onset of interstitial keratitis is typically between 6 and 12 years of age. The keratitis pre­sents as rapidly progressive corneal edema, followed by abnormal vascularization in the deep stroma adjacent to Descemet membrane. The cornea may assume a salmon-­pink color ­because of intense vascularization, giving rise to the term salmon patch. Over several weeks to months, blood flow through ­these vessels gradually ceases, leaving empty ghost vessels in the corneal stroma. For further discussion of interstitial keratitis, see Chapter 13. For additional discussion of congenital syphilis, see BCSC Section 6, Pediatric Ophthalmology and Strabismus.

Clinical Pearl The presence of bilateral interstitial keratitis in an adult or child is highly likely to be secondary to congenital syphilis.

Congenital Corneal Keloid

A corneal keloid is reactive fibrous tissue proliferation that appears as a raised white mass. It is a relatively rare lesion, thought to be a fibrotic response to corneal perforation, trauma, surgery, or chronic ocular surface inflammation. Congenital corneal keloids, which are often bilateral, have been described in oculocerebrorenal (Lowe) syndrome (an X-­linked recessive disorder characterized by cataracts, renal failure, intellectual disability, and seizures), Rubinstein-­Taybi syndrome (characterized by strabismus, nasolacrimal duct obstruction, congenital glaucoma, cataract, facial abnormalities, broad thumbs and toes, and intellectual disability), and acromegaly-­cutis verticis gyrate-­corneal leukoma (Rosenthal-­Kloepfer) syndrome. Corneal keloids are not associated with cutaneous keloids except in Rubenstein-­Taybi syndrome. Autosomal dominant inheritance has been observed in Rosenthal-­Kloepfer syndrome. Corneal keloids can occur in association with cataracts, aniridia, and glaucoma and may represent a developmental anomaly with failure of normal differentiation of corneal tissue. Histologic examination reveals thick collagen bundles haphazardly arranged, with focal areas of myofibroblastic proliferation.

Limbal Dermoid

A limbal dermoid (also called epibulbar dermoid) is a benign congenital tumor that arises most frequently in the inferotemporal limbus. It is a choristoma (mass of normal tissue found in an aty­pi­cal location). The lesions are typically raised and fleshy appearing and may contain hair, sebaceous glands, sweat glands, muscle, bone, or teeth (Fig 7-9). Limbal dermoids are usually unilateral and can vary greatly in size. They occasionally extend to the central cornea, can be full thickness, and can even involve the entire anterior segment. Visual acuity ­measurement and cycloplegic refraction are impor­tant to perform, as limbal dermoids often induce astigmatism with secondary anisometropic amblyopia. Limbal dermoids are associated with Goldenhar-­Gorlin syndrome (also called Goldenhar syndrome; oculoauricuolovertebral spectrum), trisomy 8 mosaicism syndrome, and linear nevus sebaceous syndrome. Chapter 10 discusses management of limbal dermoids.

Congenital Corneal Anesthesia

Congenital corneal anesthesia is a rare, usually bilateral, condition that is challenging to recognize and often is misdiagnosed as herpes simplex virus keratitis, recurrent corneal erosion, or dry eye. Most patients do not report any symptoms but pre­sent with painless corneal opacities and sterile epithelial ulcerations during infancy or childhood. The disorder can be divided into 3 distinct groups:

  • Group I. Associated with isolated trigeminal anesthesia, which is prob­ably due to primary hypoplasia of the hindbrain.
  • Group II. Associated with mesenchymal anomalies, which include Goldenhar-­Gorlin syndrome, Möbius syndrome, and familial dysautonomia (also known as Riley-­Day syndrome). Familial dysautonomia is autosomal recessive.
  • Group III. Associated with focal brainstem signs without evidence of mesenchymal dysplasia.

A thorough systemic examination, including neuroradiologic studies, is performed to rule out associated systemic conditions. Congenital insensitivity to pain with anhidrosis, another rare autosomal recessive condition associated with congenital corneal anesthesia, is linked to pathogenic variants in NTRK1.

­There are vari­ous treatment options, including the following:

  • frequent topical lubrication
  • punctal occlusion
  • nighttime eyelid splinting
  • permanent lateral tarsorrhaphy
  • amniotic membrane transplantation
  • scleral contact lenses
  • conjunctival flap
  • corneal neurotization

Congenital Glaucoma

Primary congenital glaucoma (PCG) is evident ­either at birth or within the first 3 years of life. It is believed to be caused by dysplasia of the anterior chamber ­angle (dysgenesis and compression of the trabecular meshwork as well as an anterior insertion of the iris root) without other ocular or systemic abnormalities. PCG usually occurs sporadically but may be inherited as an autosomal recessive trait. Characteristic findings in newborns with PCG include the triad of epiphora, photophobia, and blepharospasm. External eye examination may reveal buphthalmos, with the cornea enlarging to more than 12 mm in dia­meter during the first year of life. Corneal edema due to increased intraocular pressure is pre­sent in 25% of affected infants at birth and in more than 60% by 6 months of age; it may range from mild haze to dense stromal opacification. Breaks in Descemet membrane, typically oriented horizontally or concentric with the limbus, may occur acutely as a result of corneal stretching and are known as Haab striae (Fig 7-10). In contrast, breaks in Descemet membrane caused by birth trauma are usually vertical or oblique. For additional discussion, see BCSC Section 6, Pediatric Ophthalmology and Strabismus, and Section 10, Glaucoma.

Birth Trauma

Progressive corneal edema that develops during the first few postnatal days, accompanied by vertical or oblique posterior striae, may be caused by birth trauma (Fig 7-11; see also Fig 7-10B). Ruptures occur in Descemet membrane and the corneal endothelium. They usually heal but leave a hypertrophic ridge of Descemet membrane. The left eye is affected more than the right ­because the fetus assumes a left occiput anterior position. The edema may or may not clear; if it does clear, the cornea can become edematous again at any time ­later in life. High astigmatism and amblyopia may be associated findings. Congenital glaucoma can pre­sent with similar findings and should be considered in the differential diagnosis.

Metabolic Disorders

The mucopolysaccharidoses and the mucolipidoses are disorders caused by abnormal carbohydrate and lipid metabolism. Affected patients pre­sent with corneal clouding. ­These conditions are discussed further in Chapter 10 of this volume and in BCSC Section 6, Pediatric Ophthalmology and Strabismus.
